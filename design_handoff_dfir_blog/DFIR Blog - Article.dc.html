Finding the host: DHCP in Wireshark

An IP address is a rental. Four DHCP packets tell you the hostname, the hardware and often the OS — more than the IP ever will.

CONTENTS 01  Why the IP lies 02  The DORA handshake 03  The options that talk 04  Filters that find hosts 05  Takeaway

Why the IP lies

You get handed a pcap and a question: “which machine did this?” The traffic points at 10.0.2.117 — and that answer is worth almost nothing. DHCP leases expire. By the time you write the report, 10.0.2.117 may be a different laptop entirely.

What you actually want is an identity that survives the lease: a hostname, a MAC address, a vendor. Conveniently, every machine that joins the network announces exactly that — in cleartext — the moment it asks for an address.

The DORA handshake

A lease is negotiated in four packets. Two come from the client — those are the ones that identify it.

#MESSAGEWHAT IT SAYSFROM
DDiscover“Anyone out there? Here’s who I am.”client ◂
OOffer“You can have 10.0.2.117.”server
RRequest“I’ll take it — and here’s my name again.”client ◂
AACK“It’s yours for 86400 seconds.”server

Discover and Request carry the client’s identity. Focus there.

The options that talk

The bootp payload is a list of numbered options. Three of them do most of the forensic work:

Option 12 Host Name. The machine says its own name: DESKTOP-K4R2M9, iPhone-von-Lena. Often the whole case, one field.
Option 61 Client Identifier. Usually the MAC address — and the first three bytes (the OUI) name the hardware vendor. 3c:22:fb is Apple; a “printer” with an Apple OUI is a story.
Option 55 Parameter Request List. Which options the client asks for, in which order, is an OS fingerprint. Windows, macOS and Android each have a recognizable signature — even when the hostname was scrubbed.

Filters that find hosts

In practice, three display filters get you from “a pcap” to “a machine”:

# every lease negotiation in the capture
dhcp
# who claims a specific name
dhcp.option.hostname contains "DESKTOP"
# the packets where clients identify themselves
dhcp.option.dhcp == 3  # Request

Then Statistics → Conversations ties the MAC to every flow it touched. From there the question is no longer “which IP” but “what did DESKTOP-K4R2M9 do at 03:12” — which is a question a report can answer.

Takeaway

IPs identify a moment; DHCP identifies a machine. Before you pivot on an address, spend thirty seconds on the lease that created it — hostname, MAC, vendor, OS fingerprint. It’s the cheapest attribution you’ll ever get.
